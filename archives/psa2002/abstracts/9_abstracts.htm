Laws, Possibility, and the New Instrumentalism (Symposium)

Laws would appear to provide the cornerstone to many scientific theories. Thus understanding what is involved in a generalization constituting a law and understanding the role of laws in theories provide centrally important issues in the interpretation of science. There is a wide range of strikingly different approaches to these questions, all of which have serious drawbacks and none of which command general acceptance.

The symposium will address this subject by exploring some fresh ways of thinking about the relation between laws and possibility. Many current approaches to laws start from the presumption that a law can be thought of as a generalization which holds exactly in all of a range of possible worlds, the worlds corresponding to the modality appropriate to the subject. So, to be a physical law is to be a generalization that holds exactly in all physically possible worlds. The three papers to be presented in this symposium criticize various aspects of this conception and explore alternative ways of characterizing the connection between laws and possibility, as explained in the attached abstracts. Mathias Frisch and Eric Winsberg argue that the conception of laws as delineating possible worlds is flawed and should be replaced by an instrumentalist construal of laws. Arnold Koslow shares the view that a possible worlds semantics for laws is problematic but argues that laws can nevertheless be construed as narrowing down possibilities.

An attractive aspect of the symposium will be the opportunity to explore possible common grounds in such new approaches arising from the work of philosophers with strikingly different starting points and general attitudes towards the analysis of science. Two of the contributors, Mathias Frisch and Eric Winsberg, are younger scholars who have been working within the modeling tradition of Nancy Cartwright and Ron Giere. On the other hand, Arnie Koslow is a very well established philosopher whose work on science and logic has followed more traditional lines. It is particularly interesting to see some points of possible (if I may so speak!) convergence of ideas among scholars coming from such different traditions: I will look at similarities and differences between the instrumentalist, or model building, conclusions of Frisch and Winsberg, on the one hand, and the characterization in terms of narrowing down of possibilities of Koslow on the other. I particularly look forward, in my role as commentator for the symposium, to the opportunity to examine the points of agreement and difference in the ways that all three of the symposiasts explore alternative ways of thinking about the interplay between laws and possibility.

 

Chair    Janneke van Lith (Utrecht University) 

 

"Laws and Initial Conditions"    Mathias Frisch (Northwestern University) 

In this paper I challenge the traditional distinction between laws that delineate physically possible words and initial conditions. I discuss two examples from classical electrodynamics that challenge different aspects of this distinction. The first example concerns a case of "shell-crossing" in the electrodynamics of continuous charge distributions, which prohibits global solutions to the Maxwell-Lorentz equations for many reasonable initial conditions. The second example concerns the use of an asymptotic condition in deriving an equation of motion for a charged particle that seems to express a physically contingent fact even though it is mathematically necessary for deriving the equation of motion.

 

"Laws and the Foundations of Statistical Mechanics"    Eric Winsberg (University of South Florida) 

This paper explores some connections between competing conceptions of scientific laws on the one hand, and a problem in the foundations of statistical mechanics on the other. In the paper, I examine two proposals for understanding the time asymmetry of thermodynamic phenomena. One is David Albert's recent proposal and the other is a proposal that I outline based on Hans Reichenbach's "branch systems". I sketch an argument against the former, and mount a defense of the latter by showing how to accommodate statistical mechanics to recent developments in the philosophy of scientific laws.

 

"Laws and the Reduction of Possibilities"    Arnold Koslow (City University of New York) 

The first part of this paper briefly explores and rejects three standard views of how scientific laws might be systematically connected with physical necessity or possibility.  The first concerns laws and their consequences, the second concerns the so-called counterfactual connection, and the third concerns a possible worlds construction of physical necessity as a relativized necessity.  In the second part we develop an account of an overlooked but ubiquitous notion of possibility, and, with the aid of some examples, illustrate the special way in which laws do reduce possibilities.
