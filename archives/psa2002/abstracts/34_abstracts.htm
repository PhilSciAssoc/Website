The Making of the Genetical Theory of Evolution (Symposium)

Recently, a number of philosophers have done important philosophical work on the conceptual foundations of evolutionary theory and the role of its principal architects in the early twentieth century. They have drawn upon this period in addressing a number of issues of relevance to philosophy of science and philosophy of biology more generally, such as, the nature of scientific controversy, the role of mathematics in biological thought, the nature of biological explanations, and whether or how biology is a unified or "pluralistic" science. This symposium brings together a group of philosophers of biology who have staked out novel positions on these issues.

When R. A. Fisher, Sewall Wright, and J.B.S. Haldane - three major contributors to the development of the mathematical theory of evolution in the 1920's and 30's - first began their work, the aims and scope of the theory had not yet been decided upon. Arguably, even today, these aims are still being negotiated. Debates among evolutionary biologists about the results, the role and the limitations of population genetic theory fuel a significant part of the disagreements in the evolution literature. Thus, it is useful to return to some of the sources of these controversies. In the early 1900's the details of the genetic bases of heredity, the relationship between the hereditary material and observable variation, as well as which factors played a significant role in evolution, and how these factors were related, were as yet unknown. Fisher, Haldane and Wright were thus were defining a discipline, reacting to the Darwinian Biometrical and Mendelian schools that came before them, and bringing to bear their own notions of what kinds of problems required solution and how to picture evolution in populations. Many of the questions they sought to answer were beyond the scope of the available mathematics, many of their arguments were verbal, or appealed to metaphor or analogy, and knowledge of the range of the basic variables they treated was limited. Even today, empirical information on the size of populations, migration rates, selective values, linkage arrangements and inbreeding coefficients is incomplete. The object of this session will be a philosophical examination of these three figures and their contribution to the synthesis of Darwinism and Mendelism. There has been some controversy over what the synthesis consisted in; clearly these very different men had different conceptions of evolution. How then did their work lead to a synthesis, if at all? Did they provide a "unified" explanation of evolution? Or, is it better to think of these authors as founding a pluralistic tradition in evolutionary theory? These are some of the questions we will examine.

"The Heuristic Role of Sewall Wright’s 1932 Adaptive Landscape Diagram"    Robert A. Skipper (University of Cincinnati) 

Sewall Wright's adaptive landscape is one of the most influential heuristics in the history of evolutionary biology. Wright's biographer, Provine, harshly criticized Wright's adaptive landscape, claiming that its heuristic value is dubious because of deep flaws. Ruse has defended Wright against Provine's charges. Ruse claims that the landscape metaphor is heuristically valuable even if Wright's version of it flawed and, anyway, Provine has not shown that Wright's version of the metaphor is flawed. I argue that both Provine's and Ruse's analyses of the adaptive landscape are defective and suggest a more adequate understanding of the metaphor.

"Population Genetics and Population Thinking: Mathematics and the Role of the Individual"    Margaret Morrison (University of Toronto) 

R.A. Fisher's work on the on the compatibility of Mendelism and Darwinism is a brilliant application of mathematical and statistical techniques to evolutionary theory. Fisher himself drew the analogy between the methodology in his own work and statistical mechanics. Specifically he wanted to show that an exact specification of individual factors relevant to the population was unnecessary for arriving at a proper statistical analysis. When factors were sufficiently numerous the most general assumptions with respect to individual peculiarities would lead to the same statistical result. In gas theory it was possible to "make the most varied assumptions as to accidental circumstances, and even the essential nature of the individual molecules, and yet develop general laws as to the behaviour of gases, leaving but a few fundamental constants to be determined by experiment" (Fisher, 1922). So too with biological populations. One needed only general statistical laws about the interactions among genes rather than specific knowledge of biological individuals in order to determine the effects of evolutionary mechanisms. Mayr (1982) and other biologists have criticized the founders of mathematical population genetics for oversimplifying, for the sake of mathematical tractability, the factors that enter into the computations. The notion of a 'population' used by Fisher and even Pearson before him is thought to be not only essentialist but unable to take account of the individual in biological explanations. In this paper I examine the various ways in which the notion of an 'individual' is represented in the work of Fisher as well as in the biometric methods of Karl Pearson. I argue that it was only by neglecting certain aspects of individuals that Fisher was successful in showing how selection operated in Mendelian populations. Hence, instead of being considered as defects, the criticisms levied by Mayr against Fisher turn out to be the very source of his overwhelming success.

 

"Pluralism in Biological Explanation"    Anya Plutynski (University of Utah) 

The recent literature in philosophy of biology and philosophy of science more generally has drawn attention to the pluralism of biological explanation - we have explanations at different levels or units of selection, micro- and macro- explanations, molecular, biomedical, and evolutionary explanations. Do these explanations all have a common structure or end? The object of this paper will be to investigate whether there are a plurality of types or kinds of explanation in biology, and in particular, in the field of theoretical population genetics. Part of this will involve examining the presuppositions of different founders of theoretical population genetics - Fisher, Haldane and Wright - about the object of explanation and what most requires explaining. Further, this investigation into explanatory pluralism in population genetics will help to answer questions about whether and how we ought to regard evolutionary theory as a "unified" discipline. I will argue that the different explanations they offered had to do with their competing ideals of what a science of evolution would be, as well as their different presuppositions about the object of inquiry.

 

"Evolutionary Theory in the Twenties: The Nature of the 'Synthesis'"    Sahotra Sarkar (University of Texas at Austin) [full text]

This paper analyzes the development of evolutionary theory in the period from 1918 to 1932. It argues that: (i) Fisher’s work in 1918 constitutes a not fully satisfactory reduction of biometry to Mendelism; (ii) that there was a synthesis in the 1920s but that this synthesis was mainly one of classical genetics with population genetics, with Haldane’s Causes of Evolution being its founding document; (iii) the most important achievement of the models of theoretical population genetics was to show that natural selection sufficed as a mechanism for evolution; (iv) Haldane formulated a prospective evolutionary theory in the 1920s whereas Fisher and Wright formulated retrospective theories of evolutionary history; and (v) in the context of the history of evolutionary biology, the differences between Fisher, Haldane, and Wright are as important as their similarities.
