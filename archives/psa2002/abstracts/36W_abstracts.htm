Structuralist Approaches to Quantum Gravity (Workshop)

In recent years there has been growing interest in the philosophical foundations of quantum gravity (as evidenced by the Callendar and Huggett volume, Physics Meets Philosophy at the Planck Scale, as well as the recent one day conference, ‘Philosophical Reflections on Quantum Gravity’ (July 4th, 2001), at the University of Leeds, to give just two recent examples). As is well known, the construction of such a theory faces a number of deep-rooted problems in effectively 'bringing together' quantum theory, with its presupposed fixed space-time background, and general relativity, understood as a theory of (dynamical) space-time structure. One can approach these problems from various directions, not only from those of the differing perspectives of quantum and relativistic physics, but also those that might be described as broadly philosophical and physical.       

Thus one approach in the field proceeds through the construction of some kind of background independent quantum theory of cosmology. Baez, for example, has argued that background free quantum theories such as topological quantum field theory suggest an important analogy between the mathematics of space-time and the mathematics of quantum physics. This analogy cam then be made precise using the tools of higher-dimensional algebra and category theory in particular. As Baez points out, a further obstacle to such developments concerns our understanding of the notion of causal structure within this context. This is an issue which has been addressed by Smolin who has further explored various features of the framework of a background independent quantum theory of cosmology.

Proceeding from the perspective of General Relativity, another mode of entry into these issues is to focus on the nature of general covariance in order to understand the sense in which space-time is treated as dynamical. This raises the attendant issue of the ontological status of space-time points and on this issue, of course, philosophers of physics have been particularly active. Stachel, in particular, has argued that the infamous hole argument is invalid in cases where the individuality of space-time points is conferred by the relational structure of space-time. Adopting the kind of structuralist framework this approach suggests raises the possibility of being able to side-step the substantivalist-relationist debate and develop a new perspective on the spatio-temporal foundations of quantum gravity. Again, the most convenient mathematical framework for such a structuralist perspective would be category theory and here at Leeds we have been exploring the different ways these various frameworks and perspectives intersect.

The aim of this symposium is to bring philosophically-minded physicists and philosophers of physics together to both map out the current terrain and suggest new avenues for future exploration.

 

Chair    Steven French (University of Leeds) 

 

"Quantum Gravity, Spacetime Structures and Structural Realism"    Dean Rickles (University of Leeds) and Juha Saatsi (University of Leeds) 

Structural realism emphasises (mathematical) structures of scientific theories as representing the unobservable part of reality. This position dovetails well with much of the recent work in canonical quantum gravity, and in this paper we consider the work of Baez, Smolin et al. from this perspective. After giving a brief introduction / independent motivation to structural realism, we consider how a metaphysical notion of structure appropriate to structural realism meshes with the more intuitive notions of theoretical / spacetime structures used in this branch of quantum gravity program. It is suggested that the mode of reasoning embraced in the field is highly consonant with that recommended by structural realism, and some possible ramifications of adopting structural realism as an appropriate background philosophy to further research in this field are considered.

 

"Structure, Individuality and Quantum Gravity"    John Stachel (Boston University) 

After discussing various versions of "structural realism," I opt for a version in which the fundamental physical entities of various kinds and their relations are taken to be real. While these entities have "quiddity"(a basic nature), reasons are given for assuming that they lack "haeccity" (inherent individuality), and that all properties that do not inhere in their nature are relational. Mathematical techniques for implementing this viewpoint in a physical theory are discussed, and various formalisms that have been used in the attempt to set up a theory (or theories) of quantum gravity are evaluated on the basis of this criterion.

 

"Quantum Gravity at the Background Independent Level"    Lee Smolin (Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics) 

A general framework for quantum theories of gravity which is independent of any classical background spacetime has been worked out over the last 15 years. Known as loop quantum gravity, this framework was derived originally for quantum general relativity but it can be extended to a large class of theories including supergravity theories and plausibly candidates for unified theories such as string theories. We describe the basic structure of these theories, with attention given to issues of interest for philosophers of science such as the relational/absolute debate and the interpretation of quantum theory and quantum cosmology.

 

"Higher-Dimensional Algebra and Quantum Gravity"    John Baez (University of California, Riverside) 

The problems of quantum gravity suggest that we need a new mathematical language which exhibits the common ground between our usual ways of talking about space and time in general relativity and our usual ways of talking about states and operators in quantum mechanics. I argue that this language is already being developed by mathematicians working on higher-dimensional algebra --- or more specifically, the theory of `n-categories with duals'. While still in its infancy, this theory already clarifies how some famously puzzling features of quantum mechanics are closely akin to some not-at-all-puzzling features of spacetime. If there is time, I will also describe some work on theories of quantum gravity formulated using higher-dimensional algebra.
