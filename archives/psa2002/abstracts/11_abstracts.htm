Biological Kinds and Human Kinds (Symposium)

The 1990s saw two prominent proposals for the biological basis of human nature. Some proponents of the Human Genome Project suggested that human nature lies in our unique gene sequence. Evolutionary psychologists argued that human nature lies in a set of mental modules selected in our evolutionary past. These approaches to human nature have been widely criticised. One objection is that such approaches inappropriately adopt genetic determinism. Another objection is that such approaches rely on outdated assumptions in biological taxonomy, namely essentialism and typological thinking. There are many other criticisms.

The aim of this session is not to further criticise such accounts of human nature but to develop positive alternatives. Many philosophers and scientists assume that knowledge of our biology is important for understanding human nature. The question is how to construct a biological approach to human nature? Recent literature on biological kinds and human kinds suggests a path. That literature contains two overlapping approaches. One approach is found in Ian Hacking's work on human kinds. Human kinds are different than standard natural kinds; their etiology is both biological and sociological. The other approach to human kinds comes from evolutionary developmental theory. The etiologies of individual humans and human kinds are the result of our genes, environments, and cultures. These two approaches suggest a more robust account of the biological basis of human nature. Developmental and cultural factors, as well as genes, are seen as important components of our natures. Phylogeny weaves those different components together. This account of humans avoids the pitfalls of previous biological approaches, and it offers a richer explanatory framework for human nature. The assumption that there is a human essence is replaced with the idea that there is an evolving variety of humans. The purpose of this symposium, then, is to explore such an account of human nature and human kinds.

"Human Kinds and Biological Kinds: Some Similarities and Differences"    John Dupre' (University of Exeter) 

We constantly assign humans to different categories, just as we have elaborate systems for the classification of animals. There are some major differences between these practices, one of which is that in biology we have a need for a general system of classification. In the human case this is neither needed nor desirable. My talk will explore the consequences of this and other differences as part of a general project of exploring the heterogeneity of kinds.

 

"Emotions as Natural and Normative Kinds"    Paul Griffiths (University of Pittsburgh) [full text]

I have argued elsewhere that emotions are not a natural kind, and that the same is probably true of many specific emotion categories, such as anger and love. Many philosophers of emotion remain unconvinced. Many suppose that my position can be refuted by giving any reasonably clear analysis of vernacular concept of emotion. But the claim that emotion is not a natural kind is entirely consistent with the fact that a systematic account can be given of the vernacular concept. The question at issue is whether that vernacular category is a productive object of scientific enquiry. Other critics have been concerned to show that the category of emotion has a valuable role in normative practices such as self-criticism, and have suggested that the concept is thereby insulated from revision of intension and extension as a result of empirical inquiry. This critique raises important issues about the relationship between scientific and vernacular discourse about the mind, issues which I attempt to address here.

 

"Bridging the Gap Between Human Kinds and Biological Kinds"    Marc Ereshefsky (University of Calgary) 

Many writers claim that human kinds are significantly different from biological and natural kinds.  Some suggest that humans kinds are unique because social structures are essential for the etiology of human kinds.  Others argue that human cultural evolution is a decidedly different form of evolution.  In this paper I suggest that the gulf between humans and our biological relatives is not as wide as some argue.  There is a taxonomic difference between human and non human organisms, but such factors as social structure and cultural evolution do not distinguish us from many other organisms.
