Counterfactuals and Causality (Workshop)

Until very recently, the best philosophical literature on causality assumed without question that there is an intimate, if not essential, connection between counterfactual conditionals and causal explanations. Covering law accounts treated causal explanations as derivations of canonical descriptions of effects from descriptions of initial conditions and lawlike generalizations whose explanatory value was supposed to depend upon the ability of the lawlike generalizations to support counterfactual conditionals. David Lewis and Jim Woodward rejected this account, without questioning whether questioning the importance of counterfactual conditionals. For Lewis, the adequacy of a causal claim depends upon the truth of a counterfactual conditional whose truth value is decided by what goes on in the most accessible possible world(s) which do not include the putative cause. For Woodward the adequacy of a causal explanation depends upon the upon the truth of counterfactual conditionals about what would have resulted from ideal manipulations of putative cause. This illustrates how accounts of causal explanation have come and gone while the importance of counterfactual conditionals remained unquestioned.

Are counterfactual conditionals well enough behaved, and well enough understood to help us understand causality and causal explanation? Lewis and Stalnaker provided an elegant possible world semantics for counterfactuals. But persisting difficulties with regard to these conditionals show that that semantics alone isn't enough to relieve important doubts about them. It has seemed to some that Lewis' counterfactual account of causality cannot be applied to particular cases without arbitrary assumptions about the structures of the closest possible worlds. The epistemology of counterfactual claims remains clouded. And Nuel Belnap's and his associates' theory of branching space times (BST) provides reason to doubt whether even probabilistic counterfactual conditional claims about indeterministic systems have truth values where the received accounts of causal explanation need them.

In view of such difficulties, would be nice if causal explanation could be accounted for without appeal to counterfactual conditionals. One of the attractions of the Mechanistic theory of explanation of Machamer, Darden, and Craver ['Thinking About Mechanisms', Philosophy of Science, March, 2000] is that it attempts to do just that. Machamer, Darden, and Craver understand causally productive relations in terms of the contributions made by the activities of the parts of a system which produces an effect to that effect's production. But Woodward [PSA Symposia, 2000 forthcoming] argues that the difference between causally productive and causally irrelevant activities cannot be understood without appeal to counterfactual conditionals.

This background gives rise to the issues to be taken up in this proposed symposium. They center around the questions of whether counterfactual conditionals can be dispensed with in accounts of causality, and whether there is good reason to think they should be. And because BST gives rise to serious obstacles to counterfactual based accounts of causal explanations of non-deterministically produced effects, the proposed symposium includes a preliminary discussion of the place of causality in branching space-time.

"A Theory of Causation: Causae Causantes (Originating Causes) As Inus Conditions In Branching Space-Times"    Nuel Belnap (University of Pittsburgh) [full text]

Branching space-times (BST) theory, as developed elsewhere, permits a sound and rigorously definable notion of ``originating cause'' or causa causans---a type of transition event---of an outcome event. Mackie has famously suggested that causes form a family of ``inus'' conditions, where an inus condition is ``an insufficient but non-redundant part of an unnecessary sufficient condition.'' In this essay the needed concepts of BST theory are developed in detail, and it is then proved that the causae causantes a given outcome event have exactly the structure of a set of Mackie inus conditions. The proof requires the assumption that there is no EPR-like ``funny business.'' This seems enough to constitute a theory of ``causation'' in at least one of its many senses.

 

"Kicking the Counterfactual Habit"    Jim Bogen (University of Pittsburgh) [full text]

Nuel Belnap and the co-authors of Facing the Future use BST semantics to argue that conditional predictions about indeterminate systems (e.g., ‘if Jones decides to go to the market, then he will meet someone who owes him money’) are neither true nor false at the time they are made. Related considerations argue for the same conclusion with regard to the kinds of counterfactual conditionals Woodward thinks we need to distinguish causally productive from causally irrelevant relations, as well as the kinds of counterfactuals David Lewis relies on in his account of causality. After setting out the arguments for this, I will make a few suggestions about the kinds of conditionals which I think are involved in causal explanation.

 

"Mechanisms and Activities"    Peter Machamer (University of Pittsburgh) [full text]

Activities are what relate entities within a mechanism. Activities refer to what entities do, and how, in so doing, they affect other entities and activities. It is often unwarrantedly assumed that the kinds of entities are ontologically or epistemically prior to activities. Mechanisms are the way to spell out causal relations. What activities are working determines if they are actually causally relevant. Establishing mechanisms characterizes Leibnizian necessity, the closing off of possibilities. This is the only necessity.

 

"No Counterfactual Conditionals, No Causal Explanations!"    Jim Woodward (California Institute of Technology) [full text]

This paper defends the claim that counterfactuals with certain features play a central role in causal explanation. These are counterfactuals that describe the outcomes of hypothetical experiments. I compare my treatment to some influential alternative accounts and also reply to objections, including those raised by Bogen in this workshop.
