Wesley C. Salmon 1925-2001:A Symposium Honoring His Contributions to the Philosophy of Science (Symposium)

Wesley Salmon made many seminal contributions to the philosophy of science and the work he has left us constitutes a rich resource for research in many areas. When I was asked to organize this symposium, Merrilee Salmon suggested that to continue the spirit of Wes’s progressive philosophical outlook, the symposiasts should be asked to present papers that provided new insights into various areas in which Wes had worked, rather than providing a retrospective survey of his contributions. Because his work covers such a wide area, I have invited participants who will address three of the topics to which he devoted considerable attention for most of his career: spacetime, probability and induction, and causality and explanation. Inevitably, we shall have to neglect other areas to which Wes made important contributions and there are many other eminent philosophers of science whom I wish could have been included, but the speakers and topics chosen should provide an informed and substantive set of talks that will demonstrate the vitality of Salmon’s contributions to our discipline.

 

Chair    Adolf Grunbaum (University of Pittsburgh) 

 

"Causation and Misconnections"    Phil Dowe (University of Tasmania) 

In this paper I show how the conserved quantity theory of Wesley Salmon and myself provides a sufficient condition in an analysis of causation. To do so I will show how it handles the problem of misconnections. Take any two causally independent events, say, my tapping the table and the clock hand moving a moment later. There is no causal connection between these two particular events, but there is in fact a set of causal processes and interactions between them, according to the conserved quantity theory. The reason is that there are air molecules filling the gap between, so that one can connect the two events by stringing together a series of molecular collisions. Also there is a longitudinal disturbance, the sound of my tapping, which reaches the clock hand before it moves. So, again, there can be a set of causal processes and interactions where there is no causal connection. I set out the conditions under which a set of processes and interactions constitute a causal relation.

 

"Causal Processes: What Are They, and What Are They Good For?"    Chris Hitchcock (California Institute of Technology) 

Concerning any object of philosophical analysis, we can ask the two questions posed in the title of this talk. Despite difficulties in formulating a precise criterion to distinguish causal processes from pseudoprocesses, I will argue that Salmon answered the first of these questions with extraordinary clarity. The second question, by contrast, has not been successfully answered, by Salmon, or by anybody else. In particular, it is not clear how causal processes and interactions can be used to solve the problem of explanatory irrelevance that led Salmon to think about causation in the first place.

 

"Objective Probabilities: Frequencies and Physical Chances"    Paul Humphreys (University of Virginia) 

The frequency interpretation of probability played a central role in Wes Salmon’s philosophy of science for most of his career. Although he suggested in his later work that the propensity account could serve an important function within his theory of causation, he never rejected his earlier commitments to objectively homogeneous reference classes as a correct basis for understanding probability. Yet Wes was famous for having advocated a shift to ontic accounts of causation, a shift that has consequences reaching far beyond explanation, and this shift raises the question of how to provide a coherent account of probability that can support Salmon’s later views on probabilistic causation and explanation, views that seem more naturally suited to physical probabilities than to frequencies. In the spirit of the path that Wes’s work has opened, I shall explore the connections between the propensity and frequency accounts of probability and suggest that, just as Wes rather startlingly argued that a theory of causation need be only contingently true of our world, so too can a theory of probability that accompanies it. I shall also discuss how this shift affects our theories of inductive inference.  

 

"Spacetime and Conventionalism"    Larry Sklar (University of Michigan) [full text]

My talk would be a discussion of the basic structure of theses of conventionalism (and theses opposed to conventionalism) in spacetime theories. I would be exploring the way in which basic philosophical assumptions, of an epistemological nature, interact with the specific contents of particular spacetime theories in the conventionalist's (and anti-conventionalist's) arguments.
