Is Methodology Gendered - and Should It Be? (Symposium)

There has been a lively, on-going discussion of feminist philosophy of science and feminist epistemology amongst philosophers of science. An early influential work is the 1983 anthology edited by Sandra Harding and Merrill Hintikka, Discovering Reality : Feminist Perspectives on Epistemology, Metaphysics, Methodology, and Philosophy of Science. The most recent book to arrive on my desk is Janet Kourany’s The Gender of Science, with a 2002 copyright date. When the editors of the British Journal of Philosophy of Science commissioned survey articles for their special Millennium-2000 issue, one of the themes they wanted covered was Feminist Philosophy of Science. (See Brit. J. Phil. Sci. 51 (2000).)

The discussion so far has centered on feminist challenges to traditional philosophical discussions of objectivity, theory-ladenness, underdermination, realism, and the classic distinction between facts and values. Philosophers of science have, by and large, paid much less attention to the detailed case studies purporting to show that gender plays a major role in all facets of scientific inquiry. However, it has now become standard practice in many areas of the philosophy of scientific methodology to use case studies not only to illustrate philosophical theses but also to provide naturalistic support for them. Feminists have relied heavily on such examples, not only to argue for the possibility that gender plays a role but also to intimate that the influence of gender is ubiquitous.

In this symposium we will look in detail at some of the most interesting recent case studies and evaluate what they suggest about the role of gender in science.

 

Chair    Miriam Solomon (Temple University) 

 

"Robert Boyle, Chastity, and the Masculine Methods of Science"    Rose-Mary Sargent (Merrimack College) [full text]

In her recent case study, Elizabeth Potter attempts to show how Boyle’s experimental method was gender biased. Part of her argument focuses on the combination of the “invisibility” of women in Boyle’s published work together with his unpublished comments on female chastity, and part concerns Boyle’s rejection of the animistic explanation of his air pump experiments by Francis Line. I argue that the historical and biographical elements of the case make at least part of Potter’s argument questionable. In addition, I address directly whether and how such historical cases can shed light on current debates about gender issues and methodology in the philosophy of science.

 

"How Might We Put Our Politics Where Our Science Is?"    Noretta Koertge (Indiana University) 

Feminist analyses of science have argued that the methodology of science does not succeed in filtering out the effects of gender stereotypes and that the result is often knowledge claims that contribute to the oppression of women. However, there have been quite divergent feminist reactions to such a conclusion. One response is to propose ways of strengthening the methods of science in the hopes of removing gender bias. Another strategy is to try to prevent harmful scientific results by making the role of social values in science more positive and more salient. This paper looks at various articulations of the second response, including Fausto-Sterling’s argument that revising our binary concepts of sex and gender would not not only improve the lot of intersexed and transgendered people in our society but would also improve science. I use this example as an interesting challenge to the accounts of the proper role of social values in science offered by Giere, Longino and Kitcher.

 

"Should Science Have Gender?"    Cassandra Pinnick (Western Kentucky University) 

The best feminist critique, by which I mean those critical discussions based on arguments that are well-formulated and at least on their face appear to present a serious challenge to the accepted foundations of scientific knowledge, claims not to question either the possibility of, or desirability of, scientific rationality. Yet in the end the best of the feminist critique relies on decidedly a-rational means in its arguments for preferring a new approach to science. And despite claims to be an epistemology of science, the best feminist critique manages to be no more than a description of what is believed, rather than a normative account of what should be believed or why some belief is justified.
