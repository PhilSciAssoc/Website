Realization and Explanation in Neuroscience (Symposium)

Neuroscience has experienced explosive growth over the last 30 years. Membership in the Society for Neuroscience has increased from 500 in 1970 to 35,000 at present, and the field now rests on a bedrock of theoretical advances in understanding the chemical, electrical and physiological workings of the central nervous system. Yet the philosophy of science has largely ignored neuroscience. This is unfortunate since, first, there is considerable overlap between issues in the philosophy of neuroscience and issues in the philosophy of biology (much of neuroscience is, after all, biology) and, second, neuroscience offers unique puzzles resulting from its (partial) focus on questions of the mind. In an effort to develop bridges between the philosophy of neuroscience and the philosophy of science generally, we propose a session on realization and explanation in neuroscience.

It is frequently said that cognitive states and processes are "realized" in the mechanisms of the brain. The notion of "realization" was originally introduced by Putnam to describe the relationship between the "logical" and the "structural" states of a computer. He later wielded the notion as part of a multiple realizability argument against type-type reduction generally. This latter argument has become a centerpiece of discussions of reductionism and antireductionism in both the philosophy of mind and in the philosophy of biology.

The notion of realization has also assumed a central place in discussions of the status of higher-level generalizations in the so-called "special sciences". For many, the legitimacy of higher-level causal claims hinges upon the existence of lower-level realizers. Thus Stuart Glennan (1996) has argued that to say that one thing causes another (except for lowest level causal relations governed by direct causal laws) just is to say that the relation between them is realized by a mechanism linking cause and effect. Similar positions have been endorsed in the philosophy of neuroscience by Bill Bechtel, Terry Horgan, Ernest Lepore and Barry Loewer (1989) and Jaegwan Kim (see, e.g., 1995, 197). For many of these philosophers, the notion of "realization" ads much needed bite to logical notions of "supervenience" and thus becomes a crucial component in spelling out a robust form of physicalism.

Surprisingly, however, there is almost no consensus (or even, until quite recently, discussion) as to what it takes for one entity, state or process to realize another, about how philosophical and scientific senses of realization are related to one another, or about how the goal of discovering "realizers" shapes scientific practice. These are the questions to be addressed in our session. Craver develops an account of "realization" from his work on mechanisms and then uses it to criticize an argument by V.S. Ramachandran (the Oliver Sachs of the 90's) for the nonexistence of selves. Wilson compares such mechanistic accounts of realization to more metaphysical approaches, arguing that an emphasis on the former tends to reinforce unhealthy localistic biases in contemporary neuroscientific research. Von Eckardt disagrees, arguing that metaphysical approaches to realization have little to say about the notion of realization that is commonplace in neuroscientific research and so ought to be abandoned in favor of a more scientifically adequate notion. Polger, finally, uses Marr's theory of early visual processing to argue that a metaphysically robust notion of realization is necessary to assess the adequacy of physicalism in contemporary neuroscience.

"Mechanisms, Realization and the Self (or Self Realization)"    Carl F. Craver (Washington University) 

Recent work on the character of mechanisms and of mechanistic levels provides the basis for an account of one sort of realization relation common in neuroscience. In neuroscientific theories, roles (R) are typically realized by mechanisms (M). This realization relation has three parts: the context C within which M realizes R, the "input-output" relation by which M realizes R, and the organized activities of the entities composing M by virtue of which M maintains that input-output relation. This account of realization provides leverage to answer three questions: (i) What are the identity conditions of Rs? (ii) What are the identity conditions of Ms? and (iii) How are the identity conditions for M's and R's related to one another? These questions are raised in the context of the neurologist V.S. Ramachandran's recent attempt to argue that "the self is a fiction" on the grounds that what we call "the self" is in fact realized by several distinct neural mechanisms. I claim, on the basis of the above account of realization and the resulting take on identity conditions, that Ramachandran's argument is deeply flawed (even if his conclusion may be largely correct).

 

"Mechanisms and Explanation in Neuroscience and Psychology"    Barbara Von Eckardt (University of Nebraska-Lincoln) 

The aim of this paper is to explore, first, the merits of the mechanism approach in the philosophy of biology for understanding explanation in cognitive neuroscience and, second, the scope and limits of cognitive neuroscience itself for understanding human psychology. We shall argue that the mechanism approach, as one would expect, can capture the mechanistic aspects of cognitive neuroscientific models quite well but cannot capture content-related aspects. Furthermore, despite its promise, there are aspects of cognition and behavior that cognitive neuroscience cannot explain, specifically, those involving the social environment. Both these points become evident when one considers cases of psychopathology.

 

"Realization and Localistic Biases in Cognitive Neuroscience"    Rob Wilson (University of Alberta) 

Realization has remained a much used and seldom analyzed concept in the philosophy of psychology and, to a lesser extent, the philosophy of biology, at least until the recent appearance of a flurry of papers (e.g., Bechtel and Mundale 1999; Craver 2001; Lyons 2001; Wilson 2001). After some discussion of the differences between the more metaphysically-rarified and the more mechanistically-driven strands of these discussions, this paper focuses on the relationship between realization (or implementation) and a variety of localistic biases in contemporary research in the neurosciences. William Uttal (2001) has issued a caution about the "new phrenology" thriving in current imaging-driven research, particularly in cognitive neuroscience, and this paper will pick up on some of these concerns, relating them to recent mechanistically-driven strands in the discussion of realization.

 

"Neural Machinery and Realization"    Thomas W. Polger (University of Cincinnati) 

The view that the relationship between minds and brains can be thought of on the model of software and hardware is pervasive. The most common versions of the view, known as functionalism in philosophy of mind, hold that minds are realized by brains. The question arises, How do brains realize minds? I approach the question of realization through a case study: David Marr's (1982) computational account of early visual processing. Marr's work is instructive because it is, quite literally, a textbook case of the hierarchy of mechanisms that has seemed to bear out the arguments of functionalist philosophers and cognitive scientists.
