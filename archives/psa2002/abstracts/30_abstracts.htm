Causation & Bayesian Networks (Symposium)

Bayesian networks are the generalized nonlinear versions of Sewall Wright's path models. Bayes nets have gained ground in the artificial intelligence and business communities for their ability to represent and compute efficiently the probabilistic dependencies necessary for reasoning under uncertainty. What makes Bayes nets most compelling however is their close connection with causal inference and causation.

Following Judea Pearl's advances in the graph-theoretic underpinnings of probabilistic independence, researchers at CMU, UCLA, Monash, Microsoft, and a few other centers have developed algorithms for reasoning about and representing causality and causal manipulation using Bayes nets, and for automatically inferring causal structure from statistical data using the constraints implied by graph theory. The best known program for automated causal inference may be CMU's TETRAD II.

More recently, Clark Glymour has teamed up with Patricia Cheng and Alison Gopnik and by applying Bayes nets they have drastically improved psychological theories of human causal inference and causal reasoning. Working in parallel, researchers at Stanford have shown how human causal inference can be modeled as Bayesian inference about Bayesian networks.

Perhaps most interesting for philosophers, Pearl's recent book Causality builds on the insight from both his research team and that at CMU and engages the philosophical literature on causation with surprising insights about not only type causation but token causation and causal processes.

There is no doubt that Bayes nets provide a much more powerful representation for reasoning about causation than is commonly used in philosophy. While a few philosophers from Herbert Simon to Nancy Cartwright, Clark Glymour, Paul Humphries, and Gurol Irzik have been urging us to use path models all along, the full theory of probabilistic independency was not worked out until the mid-80s, and only more recently has the causal interpretation of Bayes nets been set out reasonably clearly.

The purpose of this seminar is to bring together leading researchers in Bayesian networks, drawn from philosophy, computer science, and artificial intelligence and attempt to arrive at some consensual understanding of the difficulties, insights and opportunities for Bayesian networks offer to philosophers of causation.

"Causal Diversity"    Nancy Cartwright (London School of Economics) 

Bayes nets are a powerful tool for causal inference. But they treat causality as a single monolithic notion. Recent work on counterfactuals by Judea Pearl and by students of Daria Lewis go some way to providing different tests appropriate to different types of causation. This paper will explore to what extent these attempts are successful and how they can be extended and improved on.

 

"Graphical Models, Token Causation and Processes"    Peter Menzies (Macquarie University) [full text]

Graphical models have traditionally been used in the social sciences and artificial intelligence to characterize type-causation or general causation. However, Judea Pearl in Chapter 10 of his book 'Causality' (CUP, 2000) has advanced a theory of token-causation, or actual causation as he calls it, based on the graphical models approach. Pearl introduces the notions of sustenance and a causal beam to capture the notion of a process that seems to be central to commonsense intuitions about token-causation.

In this paper I compare Pearl's graphical models approach to token-causation with other philosophical approaches -in particular, quasi-dependence and functionalist theories-that highlight the role of intrinsic processes and relations in judgements about token-causation. I examine the strengths and weaknesses of Pearl's approach in comparison with these other approaches by considering examples of pre-emption (early and late, preventive and non-preventive), symmetrical overdetermination, and trumping.

 

"Inferring Causal Structure: What Can We Know and When Can We Know It?"    Peter Spirtes (Carnegie Mellon University) 

The point of many statistical studies is to guide policy. Good guidance to policy makers requires making reliable predictions about what the effects of some policy would be, if it were to be adopted; this in turn requires at least partial knowledge about causal relationships among variables. Many policies are implemented by forcing a value on a random variable V (e.g. changing the amount of use of a medical drug that has been found to be dangerous by banning it from the market). In that case we will say that V has been manipulated. The fundamental question that I will address is "To what extent is it theoretically possible to predict the effects of a manipulation from observational samples and background knowledge of various kinds?" I will present a variety of recent results, some positive, and some negative, about the possibility of converging to correct predictions about the effects of manipulations, under a variety of senses of convergence (e.g. pointwise or uniform, etc.) and a variety of different background assumptions. (This is joint work with Jamie Robins, Larry Wasserman, Richard Scheines and Clark Glymour.)

 

"What Does the Causal Markov Condition Have to Do With Causality?"    James Woodward (California Institute of Technology) and Daniel M. Hausman (University of Wisconsin)

The causal Markov condition says roughly that, conditional on its direct causes, a variable is probabilistically independent of everything except its effects. This condition is one of the basic building blocks for Bayes-nets modeling of causation. But little has been said concerning why this condition should generally hold. We explore the extent to which the causal Markov condition can be justified as a probabilistic manifestation of a "control" or "manipulation" view of causation. The condition will be justified to the extent that conditioning on the direct causes of X mimics the effect of an intervention on X.
