The Pragmatics of Scientific Representation (Symposium)

Scientific representation is a central notion in the philosophy of science. It is the key to almost every variety of the realism / antirealism debate; it plays an important role in discussions of explanation; it affects the debates over the nature of laws, time, space and causation; and it is central to philosophical reflection on scientific modelling and theorising in general.

Philosophical reflection on the practice of scientific modeling has been experiencing a strong revival in recent times. A primary function of scientific models is often that of representing the world and our interactions with it. It is therefore surprising that the notion of representation itself has received relatively little philosophical attention. This symposium will approach representation through the practice of scientific model-building. It will concentrate on the specific question: "How do models represent?" The answers rehearsed in the symposium will emphasise the essentially pragmatic nature of scientific representation, and will display some of the consequences of the motto: "nothing is a representation unless it is used as such (by someone, somewhere)". Thus, a presupposition of the symposium will be that reductivist and dyadic accounts of representation are unfit for science. These are accounts that want to reduce representation to truth, or to similarity, or to structural identity, or to any other dyadic relation between the representation and the represented, by ignoring the specific purposes for which scientists employ representations in the practice of science.

Accordingly, the symposium papers will address two different types of issues. First, there will be philosophical analyses of pragmatist and intentional accounts of representation. Second, there will be historical case studies designed to illustrate the representational function of models in practice. Most papers in the symposium will do a bit of both.

 

Chair    R.I.G. Hughes (University of South Carolina) 

 

"How Models are Used to Represent Physical Reality"    Ron Giere (University of Minnesota) [full text]

What are models that they may be used to represent reality?  Answer: Models are objects that can be used to represent reality by exhibiting a designated similarity to physical objects.  Similarity is often criticized because anything may be similar to anything else and because there is no satisfactory general characterization of similarity.  I exploit these facts by insisting that claims of similarity between models and real objects must be accompanied by specifications of the respects and the degrees to which similarity is claimed, specifications that can only be supplied by those using the model according to their own interests.

 

"Imagination and Imaging in Model-Building"    Mary Morgan (London School of Economics) [full text]

Modelling became one of the primary tools of economic research in the 20th century and economists understand their mathematical models as giving some kind of representation of the economic world, one adequate enough to their purpose of reasoning about that world. But when we look at examples of how non-analogical models were first built in economics, both the process of making representations and aspects of the representing relation remain opaque. Like early astronomers, economists have to imagine how the hidden parts of their world are arranged and to make images, that is, create models, to represent how they work. The case of the Edgeworth Box, a widely used model in 20th century economics, provides a good example to explore the role of imagination and images in the process of making representations of the economy.

 

"An Inferential Conception of Scientific Representation"    Mauricio Suárez (University of Bristol) [full text]

I defend a deflationary conception of scientific representation, which minimally characterises it by means of two necessary conditions: representation is essentially intentional and it has the capacity to allow surrogate reasoning and inference. I then defend this conception by showing that it successfully meets the objections and difficulties that make its competitors, such as isomorphism and similarity, untenable. In addition the inferential conception explains the success of various means of representation in their appropriate domains, and it sheds light on the truth and accuracy of scientific representations.

 

"Telltale Signs: What Attention to Representation Reveals about Scientific Explanation"    Andrea Woody (University of Washington) 

This essay explores the connection between representation and scientific explanation. An "inferential" conception of representation is outlined. This framework is used to examine the role of some graphical techniques in elementary chemistry, emphasizing the inferential strengths afforded by the graphical format. I argue how recognition of these inferential capacities should have consequences for our understanding of the nature and role of explanation in this discipline - and quasi-inductively, for scientific disciplines more generally. The view endorsed makes natural connection with unificationist accounts of explanation. It also provides a rationale for why "explanatory power" typically is considered an epistemic virtue.

 

"Science as Representational and Non-representational"    Bas Van Fraassen (Princeton University) [full text]

Science represents the phenomena, and it does so by providing representations of nature as a whole. Criteria of adequacy for a representation pertain to accuracy and truth; but that representation is selective and may require distortion even in the selected parameters is an old and familiar point, intimately related to the insight that representation is intentional with adequacy relative to its particular purpose. If we add to this that observation and measurement are perspectival and that the appearances to be saved are the perspectival measurement outcomes, the question whether this "saving" is an explanatory relation in contemporary physics can provide a new focus for the realist/anti-realist debate.
