Evidencing The Book of Evidence (Workshop)

This workshop focuses on the latest book by Peter Achinstein (distinguished philosopher of science and winner of the Lakatos Award): The Book of Evidence (Oxford Univeristy Press, 2001). This book is a very substantial elaboration and modification of earlier work by Achinstein on the concept of evidence. It provides a deeper philosophical foundation for the theory and justifies and unifies his claims. One driving motivation for the work is to develop a practical concept of evidence, meaning one that will be of use to working scientists. To do so, he develops a robust concept of evidence that is much more demanding than all of the standard accounts of evidence considered in the philosophical literature. In developing it, he uses historical case studies to substantiate the claim that his notion will be of use to working scientists. He shows how it provides a better understanding of important evidential claims made by key figures in the history of science. (In the early chapters, Achinstein provides a fine historical analysis of cathode ray experiments done by Hertz and later by Thompson in developing his analysis of evidence. In later chapters, he addresses two historical questions pertaining to Perrin's evidence for molecules and more generally for scientific realism, and to Thomson's evidence for electrons, and uses his concept of evidence to provide a better understanding of Perrin's and Thomson's evidential claims.) Another driving force is his deep insight that a practical concept of evidence will also be one that can to solve many of the well-known philosophical puzzles and paradoxes of evidence. (Among those that he addresses in the last third of the book are the grue, ravens, lottery, and old-evidence paradoxes, the question of whether explanations or predictions furnish more evidential weight, and the Duhem-Quine thesis of holism.) So, the book and the symposium will be of special interest to philosophers of science.

The basic idea of Achinstein’s book is that evidence for a hypothesis must provide a good reason for believing the hypothesis. This idea makes his demands on a concept of evidence go well beyond those of the standard ones including Bayesian, boostrapping, likelihood, error-statistical, and hypothetico-deductive concepts. To ensure that his concept meshes well with the practice of science, he also insists that a reason for believing a hypothesis must be an objective rather than a subjective matter, and that the goodness of a reason for believing must be assessed on empirical rather than a priori grounds. These constraints are also at variance with many of the standard concepts.

The book attempts to develop the ideas above in terms of a probability requirement that involves the notion of an explanatory connection—it is shown in the book how this notion may be understood in terms of Achinstein’s theory of explanation, which he developed in previous writings. What is also new and likely to be controversial is his theory of objective epistemic probability, which he develops for the evidence condition. According to this theory, probability is a measure of the degree of reasonableness of belief in a proposition. This is an objective notion that is not relativized to what any actual or type of person knows or believes. It can, however, be relativized to various assumptions and special conditions, such as his “disregarding” condition (pp. 106-108). By reference to evidence, defined in this sense, various other concepts of evidence employed in the sciences are defined: ES (epistemic situation), subjective, and veridical evidence. He demonstrates that none of the standard theories of evidence is adequate to define any of these concepts, which he take to be the ones actually used in the sciences.

Two of the participants (Gimbel and Kronz) have previously published papers defending the standard positive relevance account of evidence against Achinstein's criticisms. This workshop will assess central aspects of Achinstein's own theory. Do scientists in fact employ a concept of evidence of the sort he proposes? Does the theory succeed in solving well-known philosophical problems of evidence?

"The Evolution of Peter Achinstein’s Notion of Evidence and His Resolutions to Some Problems of Confirmation Theory"    Steven Gimbel (Gettysburg College) 

The explication of “scientific evidence” in Peter Achinstein’s The Book of Evidence builds upon the hybrid probability/explanatory model he set out almost twenty-five years earlier in “The Concept of Evidence” and The Nature of Explanation. Still in place are the twin pillars of the view, a condition requiring a high posterior probability for the truth of the hypothesis and a condition requiring high probability for the existence of an explanatory connection between the hypothesis and the evidence statement. What is radically different in the view is Achinstein’s new objective epistemic interpretation of probability that separates degree of belief from probability of truth. On this new view, reasonableness of belief is what Achinstein terms a threshold concept that abandons the smoothness of probability for a discontinuous structure. Reasonableness of belief proceeds through discrete steps once a threshold value of probability has been exceeded. In The Book of Evidence, Achinstein discusses how his probabilistic conditions handle several of the classic problems of evidence, e.g., Hempel’s raven paradox, Goodman’s grue paradox, and Glymour’s problem of old evidence, in light of their recasting within the new interpretation of the probability calculus. Each of these problems has received treatment by Achinstein in previous publications. This presentation will consider how Achinstein’s new objective epistemic view of probability affects his previous solutions to these long-standing puzzles of scientific evidence.

 

"Evidence, Tactics and Strategy"    Philip Kitcher (Columbia University) 

Peter Achinstein’s new book aims to develop an account of evidence that will explain how that notion figures in scientific contexts. His treatment consists partly in critiques of rival views and partly in an articulation of his own suggestion. I’ll be concerned with the latter.

At the heart of Achinstein’s proposal is a notion of objective epistemic probability, understood as degree of reasonableness of belief. Although Achinstein says a lot of useful things about the properties any such measure should have, I think he leaves crucial lacunae that affect the ability of scientists (or reconstructing philosophers) to pin down the degree to which it’s reasonable to believe something in a particular context.

I see Achinstein as bravely defending the possibility of a formal theory of evidence. But I claim that we’re unlikely to obtain any such formal theory that will apply to the contexts in which scientific work is done. Philosophers are good at explicating the tactics of scientific argumentation. Philosophical advice about strategy is much more vague, and understandably so. Assessing evidence in a scientific context typically involves recognizing alternative possibilities, variously encumbered with difficulties and unsolved puzzles. Attempting to pin a measure on the degree of reasonableness of a belief would typically require some calculus for weighing promise against difficulty. Scientific debates occur precisely because no such calculus is available.

But scientists often can and do make qualitative estimates. The heart of a theory of evidence should consist in understanding the heuristic strategies by which they do so. In some parts of his book, Achinstein can be seen as providing useful hints towards a theory of this kind. But he is, I believe, beguiled by the philosopher’s dream of a formal theory.

 

"A Fast Track to Confirmation"    Frederick Kronz (University of Texas at Austin) and Amy McLaughlin (University of Texas at Austin) 

We provisionally accept Achinstein's theory of evidence, which essentially maintains that having evidence for a hypothesis requires having good reason for believing it. We consider what strategy best serves the search for evidence so construed. Our foundation comes from Peirce's work on the economy of research and his claim, on this basis, that efficiently gathering evidence for a hypothesis requires testing for those empirical consequences that "appear to be otherwise least likely to be true." The pragmatic developments in this paper are offered as friendly amendments to Achinstein's theory.
