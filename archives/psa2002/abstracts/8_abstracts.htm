The Epistemology of Thought Experiments (Symposium)

Content

What is the origin and nature of scientific knowledge? This question is of fundamental importance to philosophy of science. Over the millennia there have been many competing answers to the question. How are we know which are the stronger answers? The new debate over thought experiments offers us a way.

Thought experiments are one of the most remarkable instruments of scientific writing. A scientist—a Galileo, Newton, Darwin, Mach or Einstein—presents us some pressing matter in science to resolve. We are uncertain of the resolution. The scientist conjures up an experiment purely in thought. We follow its development, replicating it in our thought. When it is concluded, our uncertainty is gone. We know the resolution and sense that somehow we knew it all along.

The transformation is compelling and apparently miraculous. New knowledge appears from simple introspection. Any account of the origin and nature of scientific knowledge must be able to accommodate it. It is a telling test case. Others tests may require abstruse technicalia or obscure historical detail. The power of thought experiments is that the basic phenomena are known to us all first hand. While some thought experiments may require specialist knowledge, many are by design accessible to a broad group; and everyone has a favorite thought experiment in which the grand result leaps unexpectedly onto the stage.

Over the last decade and a half, there has arisen a new literature over the epistemology of thought experiments. The four participants in the symposium proposed are principal proponents of viewpoints that span the range of possible positions. Norton and Brown each defend what are the extremes of the traditional empiricist-rationalist axis. Norton's view is deflationary. Pure thought, he urges, can give us no more knowledge that we already have. It can only reorganize what we already know and it must do so by the only truth- (or probability-) preserving vehicle we know, argumentation. Brown however feels that empiricist epistemologies cannot do justice to the manifest power to some thought experiments to bring us new knowledge. No simple adjustment to these epistemologies could ever do justice to that power. We must seek a radical source: we learn from thought experiments when they allow our introspection to access the laws of nature in the Platonic world of forms.

While many intermediate positions and new axes are possible, two are explored by Gendler and McAllister. Gendler urges that thought experiments lead us to new knowledge by prompting a reconfiguration of internal conceptual space: contemplation of imaginary particulars may lead us to introduce novel categories for making sense of previously unsystematized beliefs. Such a view differs from Brown's rationalism in denying that such reconfiguations require appeal to platonic perceptual capacities; it differs from Norton's empiricism in rejecting argumentation as the sole source of truth-preservation.

McAllister argues that the approaches of the other contributors neglect the historical dimension of scientific practice. In his view, thought experiments are not self-evidently valid or universally applicable devices. Rather, the evidential significance of thought experiments depends on our subscribing to Galileo's view that science aims at uncovering phenomena, and that experience of events is a poor source of knowledge about phenomena. Whereas thought experiments arise out of fundamentally empiricist concerns, therefore, they embody a mistrust of experience. In domains of science inspired by Galilean physics, thought experiments constitute a valid approach to uncovering laws of nature; in other domains, they are assigned no evidential significance.

Why Now and Why This?

Why have this symposium now? While thought experiments were on the PSA program 10 years ago, this is the first symposium that would target the epistemological problems quite narrowly. There are so many other issues pertaining to thought experiments—literary, cognitive and rhetorical, for example—that it has proven all too easy to raise the hard epistemological questions and sidestep them. We will not do this. Indeed the four of us have been engaged in deep and long-standing debate that extends over a decade and a half. Yet we have never met in a public forum to debate face to face. This will be the first time!

 

Chair    Nancy J. Nersessian (Georgia Institute of Technology) 

 

"Peeking Into Plato’s Heaven"    James Robert Brown (University of Toronto) 

Naturalism and empiricism are overwhelmingly dominant. They do an adequate job in many areas of knowledge, but seem to fall short in fields such as ethics and mathematics. They also fall short in central parts of natural science. There are some thought experiments, I will argue, that cannot be adequately reconstructed along empiricist lines. To show this is to show (for the most part), that the empiricist reconstructions of John Norton leave crucial aspects of some thought experiments out of the account. It is to show, in other words, that there are some things traditional rationalists and platonists got right — a priori knowledge of nature is indeed possible. In addition to discussing thought experiments in the natural sciences, I want to extend what we have learned from them into the realm of mathematics. The aim is to show that some visualizations can yield remarkable findings, results that are just as rigorous as standard derivations. It is even possible (following Freiling’s work) that the Continuum Hypothesis could be settled with this approach. But even if not, we still can learn much about the existence of tools for acquiring knowledge that greatly transcend our feeble empirical methods.

 

"Thought Experiments: Is There More to the Argument?"    John D. Norton (University of Pittsburgh) [full text]

Thought experiments in science are merely picturesque arguments. This view enables a simple empiricist account of thought experiments. That thought experiments can err but still be used reliably shows they must conform to some generalized logic, which I claim turns out to be ordinary logic. The view is defended against alternatives proposed by my co-symposiasts.

 

"A Constructivist Account of Thought Experiments"    Tamar Szabó Gendler (Syracuse University) 

I will defend what I call a constructivist account of thought experiments. According to such an account, it is by causing us to contemplate particular imaginary scenarios that thought experiments gain their epistemic force--a force sufficient to provide us with true beliefs that are both novel and justified. The novelty of the beliefs results from our considering familiar phenomena in light of a new conceptual framework; their justification results from our gaining propositional access to previously unarticulated knowledge. In my symposium talk, I will lay out the principal claims and consequences of this view, contrasting it with the views of James Robert Brown, John Norton, and James W. McAllister.

 

"Thought Experiments and the Belief in Phenomena"    James McAllister (University of Leiden) 

Thought experiments acquire evidential significance only under particular metaphysical assumptions. These include the thesis that science aims at uncovering "phenomena", universal and stable modes into which the world is articulated, and the thesis that actual occurrences reveal phenomena imperfectly. Only under these Platonically inspired assumptions does it make sense to attempt to bypass experience of actual occurrences and perform thought experiments. These assumptions are taken to hold in post-Galilean physics, but not in sciences that emphasize contingency, such as Aristotelian physics and present-day human sciences. This explains why thought experiments carry weight in the former but not the latter disciplines.
